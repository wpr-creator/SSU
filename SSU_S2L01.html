America in World War II

Essential Question How did the attack on Pearl Harbor pull the United States into a war it had tried to avoid?

On the morning of December 7, 1941, a young sailor named James Jones was eating breakfast aboard the USS Arizona in Pearl Harbor, Hawaii. At 7:55 a.m., Japanese bombs began to fall. Within two hours, the Arizona was sinking. Jones survived. More than 1,100 of his shipmates did not. That single Sunday morning changed everything. A country that had spent years trying to stay out of the war in Europe and Asia suddenly had no choice. The United States was going to war.

Chapter One

The U.S. Stays Out: Isolationism and the Road to War

By the late 1930s, two massive wars were already underway. In Europe, Adolf Hitler had led Nazi Germany to conquer nation after nation. In Asia, Japan had invaded China and was pushing to control the entire Pacific region. Millions of people were already dying. Most Americans watched all of this from a distance — and wanted to keep it that way.

This attitude had a name: isolationismThe belief that a country should stay out of other nations' problems and focus on its own affairs.. Americans had seen their soldiers die in World War I just two decades earlier. They did not want to send their sons to die in another European war. Polls showed that most Americans were deeply opposed to getting involved.

A powerful group called the America First Committee pushed hard to keep the United States neutral. Famous people like pilot Charles Lindbergh spoke at huge rallies, arguing that the Atlantic Ocean was wide enough to protect America from the fighting overseas. Congress passed laws called the Neutrality Acts to prevent the U.S. from selling weapons to countries at war.

But President Franklin D. Roosevelt could see that staying completely out of the war was becoming harder. He believed that if Britain fell to Hitler, the United States would eventually face Germany alone. So in 1941, Roosevelt found a way to help Britain without officially entering the war: he passed the Lend-Lease Act, which allowed the U.S. to "lend" weapons and supplies to Britain. The United States was not yet a fighter, but it was no longer truly neutral either.

Meanwhile, Japan had signed a military alliance with Germany and Italy called the Axis powers. Japan wanted to build an empire across Asia and the Pacific, and American military bases in the Pacific were directly in the way. As 1941 wore on, tension between Japan and the United States grew sharper every month. Japan needed one bold move to remove the American threat. They chose Pearl Harbor.

"We must be the great arsenal of democracy. For us this is an emergency as serious as war itself. We must apply ourselves to our task with the same resolution, the same sense of urgency, the same spirit of patriotism and sacrifice as we would show were we at war."
Franklin D. Roosevelt, Fireside Chat radio address, December 29, 1940.

Roosevelt is telling Americans that even though they are not at war yet, they must act as if the emergency is just as serious — by producing weapons and supplies to help the countries already fighting.

Chapter Two

Pearl Harbor: The Day That Changed Everything

Japan's attack on Pearl HarborThe U.S. Navy base in Hawaii that Japan attacked without warning on December 7, 1941, pulling the United States into World War II. on December 7, 1941, was a military surprise. Japanese commanders chose Pearl Harbor because destroying the American Pacific Fleet there would buy Japan time to take over Southeast Asia and the Pacific islands. They sent 353 aircraft in two waves, launched from six aircraft carriers sitting 230 miles north of Hawaii.

The attack lasted about two hours. When it was over, 8 American battleships had been hit; 4 were sunk. The USS Arizona exploded and sank in less than nine minutes, killing 1,177 sailors. In total, 2,403 Americans died that morning. Another 1,178 were wounded. The entire Pacific Fleet was crippled.

The next day, December 8, President Roosevelt went before Congress. His speech lasted less than seven minutes. He called December 7 "a date which will live in infamy." Congress voted to declare war on Japan with only one dissenting vote. Three days later, Germany and Italy — Japan's Axis partners — declared war on the United States. America was now fully at war on two fronts: the Pacific and Europe.

The attack did not destroy everything Japan hoped it would. The American aircraft carriers were not at Pearl Harbor that day; they were out on exercises at sea. Those carriers would prove essential. But in the immediate aftermath, isolationism was dead. The America First Committee shut down. Charles Lindbergh urged Americans to join the military. Overnight, a deeply divided country became united behind one cause.

"Yesterday, December 7th, 1941 — a date which will live in infamy — the United States of America was suddenly and deliberately attacked by naval and air forces of the Empire of Japan."
Franklin D. Roosevelt, Address to Congress, December 8, 1941.

Roosevelt is announcing to the American people and Congress that the attack was not an accident — Japan planned it — and he is asking Congress to officially declare war.

Chapter Three

The War Effort: America Mobilizes

After Pearl Harbor, the United States had to do something it had never done before at that speed: turn a peacetime country into a war machine. Factories that made cars started making tanks and jeeps. Factories that made washing machines started making airplane parts. By 1944, the United States was producing more weapons and equipment than Germany, Japan, and Italy combined.

Millions of American men were drafted into military service. To fill the jobs they left behind, women entered the workforce by the millions. The government created a symbol for this shift: Rosie the Riveter, a woman in a factory uniform with her sleeves rolled up. Six million women took jobs in war factories during the war. Many had never worked outside the home before. This change would have lasting effects on American society long after the war ended.

The war also changed life on the home front in painful ways. The government rationed food, gasoline, rubber, and metal so that soldiers at the front had enough. Families received ration books that limited how much sugar, butter, and meat they could buy. People grew "victory gardens" in their backyards to supplement their food supply. Sacrifice was expected from everyone.

One of the darkest chapters of the war at home was the treatment of Japanese Americans. After Pearl Harbor, fear and prejudice led the government to forcibly remove 120,000 people of Japanese descent from their homes on the West Coast and send them to incarceration camps in the desert interior. Most were American citizens. They had committed no crime. Families were given days to sell everything they owned and report to assembly centers. This policy, ordered by President Roosevelt through Executive Order 9066, is now recognized as a serious violation of civil rights.

Despite this injustice, many Japanese Americans chose to serve in the U.S. military. The 442nd Regimental Combat Team, made up almost entirely of Japanese American soldiers, became one of the most decorated units in American military history. Their motto was "Go for Broke." They fought for a country that had imprisoned their families. Today, incarceration of Japanese Americans is taught as a warning about what fear and prejudice can do to a democracy. Many Japanese American families from California and the West Coast — including in and around San Diego — were directly affected.

San Diego and the Home Front: A City Transformed by War

San Diego was ground zero for the U.S. response to Pearl Harbor. The city was already home to major U.S. Navy and Marine Corps bases, and after December 7, 1941, it became one of the busiest military hubs in the entire country.

Consolidated Aircraft (now General Dynamics) in Lindbergh Field employed over 40,000 workers and built the B-24 Liberator bomber. Japanese American families in San Diego's Logan Heights neighborhood were among those forcibly removed to incarceration camps in 1942. The USS San Diego, a light cruiser, earned more battle stars than any other ship in the Pacific Fleet.

Chapter Four

Turning Points: From Midway to Victory

By mid-1942, the Allied powersThe countries that fought together against Germany, Japan, and Italy in World War II — mainly the United States, Great Britain, the Soviet Union, and France. — the United States, Britain, the Soviet Union, and others — were fighting on two opposite sides of the world at the same time. They needed a strategy to win both. They called it "Europe First": defeat Germany before turning full attention to Japan. But the war in the Pacific demanded attention immediately.

The first major turning point in the Pacific came at the Battle of Midway in June 1942. American code breakers had cracked the Japanese naval code and knew Japan was planning an attack on Midway Island. U.S. commanders set a trap. In a fierce three-day battle fought entirely in the air and at sea, the United States sank four Japanese aircraft carriers — the backbone of the Japanese naval attack force. Japan never fully recovered. The United States now had the advantage in the Pacific.

The American strategy in the Pacific was called island-hoppingA military strategy in which Allied forces captured specific key islands while bypassing others, moving closer to Japan step by step.. Instead of fighting to take every Japanese-held island, American forces selected key islands and captured them one by one, using each as a base to move closer to Japan. Battles at Guadalcanal, the Philippine Sea, Iwo Jima, and Okinawa were brutal. At Iwo Jima, marines fought for 36 days to take a volcanic island barely five miles long. More than 6,000 Americans died there.

In Europe, the tide turned with the massive Allied invasion of Normandy, France on June 6, 1944 — called D-DayThe Allied invasion of the beaches of Normandy, France on June 6, 1944 — the largest seaborne military invasion in history and a critical turning point in defeating Germany.. General Dwight D. Eisenhower commanded 156,000 American, British, and Canadian troops who landed on five beaches in a single day. The fighting was murderous: on Omaha Beach alone, around 2,000 Americans were killed or wounded in the first few hours. But the Allies held the beaches and began pushing east toward Germany.

Germany fought back hard. In December 1944, Hitler launched a last desperate offensive through the Ardennes Forest in Belgium — the Battle of the Bulge. German forces pushed 50 miles into Allied lines and surrounded the American town of Bastogne. When the German commander demanded the American soldiers surrender, General Anthony McAuliffe sent a one-word reply: "Nuts." Allied forces held on, and within weeks, they pushed Germany back. The Battle of the Bulge was Germany's last major offensive of the war.

Germany surrendered on May 8, 1945 — called V-E Day (Victory in Europe). But the war in the Pacific continued. Japan still controlled vast territory and had made clear it would fight to the last person. U.S. military planners estimated that an invasion of the Japanese mainland could cost over a million American lives. President Harry Truman made a fateful decision: use the newly developed atomic bomb. On August 6, 1945, the bomb destroyed the city of Hiroshima. Three days later, a second bomb destroyed Nagasaki. Japan announced its surrender on August 15, 1945. World War II was over.

How This Still Matters Today

The choices made during World War II shaped the world you are living in right now.

The alliances formed in WWII became the foundation for NATO, the United Nations, and the global order that still governs international conflict — including every war and crisis you see in the news today.
