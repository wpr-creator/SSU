THE NEW DEAL'S LONG SHADOW — 1938 TO TODAY

1938

Fair Labor Standards Act — 40-hour work week and minimum wage. The New Deal's final major legislation.

1945–65

New Deal coalition dominates American politics. Union membership peaks at 35% of the workforce.

1964

Barry Goldwater runs against the welfare state — losing in a landslide but building the modern conservative movement.

1980

Ronald Reagan wins on a platform of reducing government, cutting taxes, and rolling back the New Deal's regulatory framework.

Today

Social Security, FDIC, SEC, minimum wage, and unemployment insurance are still the law. The argument about their size is still ongoing.

In 1964, a senator from Arizona named Barry Goldwater ran for president on a platform that included challenging the constitutionality of Social Security. He lost to Lyndon Johnson in one of the largest landslides in American history — winning only 52 electoral votes to Johnson's 486. The American people, it seemed, had decisively answered the question of whether the New Deal's safety net should exist. But Goldwater's campaign was not the end of the argument. It was the beginning of a new phase of it. The ideas he articulated — that the federal government had grown too large, that individual liberty was being sacrificed to collective security, that the market could provide what government was trying to mandate — did not disappear with his defeat. They regrouped, organized, and came back. Sixteen years later, Ronald Reagan won the presidency on essentially the same arguments. The debate that FDR opened in 1933 has never fully closed.

CHAPTER ONE

WHAT THE NEW DEAL LEFT BEHIND

The programs that survived from the New Deal became so embedded in American life that most Americans cannot imagine the country without them. Social SecurityA federal program created in 1935 that provides retirement income to older Americans, funded by payroll taxes on workers and employers — now covering virtually all American workers. now covers nearly every American worker and their family, providing retirement income, disability benefits, and survivor benefits. The FDIC insures every bank deposit up to $250,000, making bank runs like those of 1929-1933 essentially impossible. The SEC requires public companies to disclose their finances and prohibits the kind of stock market manipulation that contributed to the 1929 crash. Unemployment insurance provides temporary income when workers lose their jobs. The 40-hour work week and overtime pay protect workers from mandatory overwork. The federal minimum wage — however contested its level — establishes that workers have a floor below which their compensation cannot fall.

DID YOU KNOW

Ronald Reagan, who spent his presidency arguing that the federal government had grown too large, was himself a union member and served as president of the Screen Actors Guild from 1947 to 1952. He was a New Deal Democrat who voted for FDR four times. His shift toward conservatism happened gradually over two decades — driven partly by his experiences fighting what he saw as communist infiltration in Hollywood. The most famous opponent of the New Deal's legacy spent his early career as one of its enthusiastic supporters.

CHAPTER THREE

THE OPPOSITION THAT NEVER STOPPED

From the moment the New Deal began, organized opposition to it also began. The American Liberty League, funded by major corporations and wealthy families, argued that the New Deal was unconstitutional, socialist, and dangerous to individual freedom. These were not fringe arguments: they were made by former Democratic presidential candidates, prominent lawyers, and major business leaders. The Supreme Court agreed with some of them, striking down several New Deal programs in 1935 and 1936. The core argument of the opposition was that the federal government had no legitimate power to regulate the economy, redistribute income, or protect workers' rights to organize — that these were matters for the market, not the state.

THE NEW DEAL ARGUMENT

The market alone cannot protect workers or prevent economic catastrophe. The Depression proved it. Government has an obligation to provide a floor under economic life — retirement security, unemployment insurance, regulated banking, a minimum wage. Liberty is not meaningful without economic security.

THE CONSERVATIVE ARGUMENT

Government programs grow beyond their original purpose, create dependency, reduce individual initiative, and require ever-increasing taxation. True liberty means freedom from government interference. Markets, if allowed to function freely, will produce better outcomes than government programs can mandate.

ART SPOTLIGHT

When the murals were unveiled in 1933, they caused an immediate controversy: critics said Rivera had included communist imagery in the panels, including a scene they claimed depicted the Holy Trinity as auto workers. A campaign to whitewash the murals began. Edsel Ford refused, saying the murals depicted American workers honestly and deserved to stand. The murals survived and are considered one of the great achievements of American public art — a monument to industrial labor paid for by the family that owned the largest automotive company in the world. Rivera was a communist. Ford was a capitalist. The murals exist because they agreed on one thing: that workers deserved to be seen.

CHAPTER FOUR

THE DEBATE THAT NEVER ENDED

The debate about the New Deal's legacyThe lasting effect or impact of a person, event, or era on the world that came after it. is not a historical argument about the 1930s. It is a live political argument happening right now about the size of the federal government, the role of unions, the level of the minimum wage, the funding of Social Security, and the extent of federal regulation of the economy. Every debate about those issues in Congress, every presidential campaign, and every state budget negotiation is a continuation of the argument that FDR started in 1933 when he said the federal government had a responsibility to the economic welfare of its citizens.

The debate about the New Deal is not just history. It is a live political argument happening right now — about the size of government, the role of unions, what the minimum wage should be, how Social Security should be funded, and how much the federal government should regulate businesses. Every budget debate in Congress, every election, and every argument about workers' rights is a continuation of the argument FDR started in 1933 when he said the federal government had a responsibility to its citizens' economic welfare.

What the New Deal permanently settled was narrower than its supporters sometimes claim and broader than its opponents acknowledge. It settled that some floor existed: that old people who worked their whole lives deserved retirement income, that workers deserved some protection from arbitrary firing, that bank deposits deserved protection, that the stock market needed oversight. It did not settle how high that floor should be, who should be covered, how it should be paid for, or how much regulation was appropriate. Those questions remain open. The specific level of the minimum wage is debated every decade. The solvency of Social Security is debated every few years. The appropriate scope of federal labor law has been contested in every Congress since 1935.

PRIMARY SOURCE MOMENT

"Government is not the solution to our problem; government is the problem... It is no coincidence that our present troubles parallel and are proportionate to the intervention and intrusion in our lives that result from unnecessary and excessive growth of government... In the days ahead I will propose removing the government's smothering hand from where it does not belong."

— Ronald Reagan, First Inaugural Address, January 20, 1981

WHY THIS MATTERS: Reagan is giving a direct answer to FDR's argument that government had a responsibility to protect Americans from economic catastrophe. FDR said the problem was the market failing and the solution was government action. Reagan says the problem is government itself. He is inaugurated 48 years after FDR's first New Deal programs — and the programs FDR built are still in place. Notice what Reagan is promising to do: not eliminate those programs, but reduce the "growth" of government. As you read this, ask: is Reagan arguing against the New Deal, or against the expansion of it after FDR?

THEN

In 1933, FDR signed into law programs that had never existed in the United States: federal retirement income, deposit insurance, stock market regulation, unemployment insurance, and worker protections. He did it because the Depression had proven that markets alone could not prevent catastrophic economic failure, and that the government had to act. He also did it imperfectly, with exclusions that left millions of Americans outside the protection he was building.

NOW

Today, Social Security serves over 70 million Americans — retirees, disabled workers, and survivors of workers who have died. The FDIC insures every bank deposit. The SEC oversees the stock market. The minimum wage and 40-hour work week are federal law. These programs outlasted every president since FDR who tried to reduce or eliminate them — including Reagan, who campaigned on reducing government but left Social Security essentially intact.

CONNECTION

The New Deal did not save American democracy in the sense of rescuing a fragile system from collapse — the democratic system itself survived the Depression, however battered. What it did was permanently change what Americans believe government is for. Before 1933, most Americans assumed the federal government had no role in protecting individuals from economic catastrophe. After 1938, that assumption was replaced by a different one: that some protection is owed. The argument today — about how much protection, who pays for it, and who deserves it — is the direct descendant of the argument FDR and his opponents had in the 1930s.

Reagan said "government is the problem." FDR said government was the solution. Both men were responding to real failures — FDR to the failure of markets, Reagan to the failure of government programs to deliver everything they promised. Which argument do you think better describes the role government should play in economic life — and what evidence would you use?
